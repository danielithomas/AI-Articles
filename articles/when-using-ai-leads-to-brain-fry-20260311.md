# When Using AI Leads to "Brain Fry"
**Authors:** Julie Bedard, Matthew Kropp, Megan Hsu, Olivia T. Karaman, Jason Hawes and Gabriella Rosen Kellerman

**Category:** Strategy & Governance

**Published:** 6 March 2026

---

## Key Points
- A BCG survey of 1,488 full-time US workers found that 14% had experienced "AI brain fry" - defined as mental fatigue from excessive use or oversight of AI tools beyond one's cognitive capacity.
- The most mentally taxing form of AI use is oversight of AI agents: high oversight levels were associated with 14% more mental effort, 12% more mental fatigue, and 19% greater information overload.
- Perceived productivity increases with each additional AI tool up to three simultaneous tools, after which productivity scores decline - indicating a cognitive ceiling effect.
- AI brain fry carries measurable business costs: affected workers reported 33% more decision fatigue, 39% more major errors, and a 39% higher intent to quit compared to unaffected colleagues.
- Leaders can reduce AI brain fry by redesigning work holistically, setting explicit expectations about workload, shifting performance metrics from AI activity to business impact, and building employees' AI workload management skills.

---

## Summary
A research team from Boston Consulting Group and the University of California, Riverside surveyed 1,488 full-time US workers to investigate the cognitive effects of different patterns of AI tool use. The study distinguishes between two distinct forms of AI-related employee distress: traditional burnout, which encompasses emotional and physical exhaustion from chronic workplace stress, and a newly identified phenomenon the authors term "AI brain fry" - acute mental fatigue arising from excessive use or oversight of AI tools beyond an individual's cognitive capacity. Crucially, while using AI to automate repetitive tasks reduces burnout by 15%, intensive AI oversight does not alleviate that stress and instead triggers a separate, acute cognitive strain.

The primary driver of AI brain fry is the oversight burden placed on employees managing semi-autonomous AI agents and tools. As firms increasingly incentivise employees to build and oversee complex teams of agents - using proxy metrics such as token consumption or lines of AI-generated code - workers find themselves responsible for monitoring more outcomes simultaneously. The study found a productivity ceiling at three simultaneous AI tools; beyond that point, the cognitive cost of multitasking and switching between tools outweighs the efficiency gains. Symptoms described by participants include mental fog, a "buzzing" feeling, difficulty focusing, slower decision-making, and headaches. Marketing roles reported the highest prevalence (26%), followed by HR, operations, engineering, finance, and IT.

The authors identify manager, team, and organisational factors that significantly moderate AI brain fry. Workers whose managers actively answer AI-related questions report 15% lower mental fatigue, while those expected to figure out AI on their own incur an "AI orphan tax" of 5% higher fatigue. At the organisational level, employees who believe their company will increase workload expectations as a result of AI report 12% higher fatigue scores, while those who feel their organisation values work-life balance report 28% lower fatigue. The authors argue that the solution is not less AI, but a holistic redesign of work - setting explicit workload expectations, shifting metrics from AI activity to business impact, and developing employees' skills in problem framing, analysis planning, and strategic prioritisation.
