# The AI Transformation Manifesto
**Authors:** Alex Singla, Alexander Sukharevsky, Eric Lamarre, Kate Smaje, Robert Levin

**Category:** Strategy & Governance

**Published:** April 2026

---

## Key Points
- Technology alone does not create competitive advantage; enduring capabilities built across talent, operating model, tech, data, and adoption are what separate AI leaders from their peers.
- Leading AI companies delivered 20 percent EBITDA uplift on average, reached breakeven in one to two years, and generated $3 of incremental EBITDA for every $1 invested by concentrating on one to three strategic business domains.
- Speed is the defining organisational advantage, requiring embedded AI talent, platform-driven reuse, and governance tied to business outcomes rather than projects.
- Every AI transformation is fundamentally a people transformation, following "30-70 shifts" for talent density while human roles shift up the value stack as AI agents handle routine work.
- Agentic engineering is identified as the next critical capability to master, with leading companies already extending platforms with agentic capabilities and codifying repeatable agentic playbooks.

---

## Summary
McKinsey presents twelve themes that separate companies successfully transforming with AI from those that are not. Drawn from the firm's work on hundreds of large-scale transformations and profiled in the second edition of their book "Rewired," the themes are organised around six foundational capabilities: strategic road mapping, talent, operating model, tech, data, and adoption at scale. The central argument is that competitive advantage does not come from AI technology itself, which is broadly available, but from how quickly and effectively companies apply it to real business problems. Each theme is framed as a diagnostic question for leaders to assess their own organisation's progress.

The first half of the manifesto addresses strategic focus and organisational readiness. Companies should concentrate AI efforts on key economic leverage points rather than pursuing long lists of use cases, as demonstrated by Freeport-McMoRan in mining yield and Toyota in supply chain integration. Senior business leaders, not IT, must drive the transformation, combining deep domain expertise with technology and AI know-how. The talent model follows "30-70 shifts": more than 70 percent of talent in-house, more than 70 percent as builder-engineers, and more than 70 percent performing at competent or expert skill levels. Speed through platform reuse, embedded teams, and reduced organisational latency is positioned as the defining advantage in an innovation race where all competitors have access to the same underlying technologies.

The second half addresses infrastructure, trust, and the emerging agentic frontier. Data must be productised and enriched as a business-owned performance asset. AI systems must be designed for adoption and built for scale from the outset, with modular architectures and coordinated central-to-local deployment rather than retrofitted later. Digital trust spanning cybersecurity, data protection, and AI transparency is framed as a prerequisite for any deployment, with the expansion of agentic technologies requiring substantially more testing and automated risk controls. The manifesto identifies agentic engineering as the next critical capability, noting that foundation models capable of sustained autonomous work are enabling complex agentic workflows, particularly in software development. Finally, continuous learning is positioned as the meta-capability underpinning all others, with CEO-led leadership learning journeys described as the single most important action to accelerate transformation.
