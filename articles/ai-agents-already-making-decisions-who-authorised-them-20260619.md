# AI Agents Are Already Making Decisions in Your Organisation. Who Authorised Them?

**Authors:** Daniel Thomas

**Category:** Strategy & Governance

**Published:** 19 May 2026

---

## Key Points
- AI agents are already embedded in organisational workflows — often inside SaaS products firms have already bought — faster than governance frameworks can be put in place, creating hidden accountability gaps.
- Governance must shift from **tool governance** (evaluating accuracy, controlling behaviour through prompts) to **partner governance** (evaluating judgment, governing through authority and constraints) once agents can perceive, decide, and act within a role.
- The **Delegation Test** is the core diagnostic: if you cannot answer the same accountability and oversight questions before deploying an agent as before hiring a person, you do not have governance — you have hope.
- Each deployed agent should be governed against eight management questions covering role definition, applicable policies, authorised decisions and authority limits, out-of-mandate escalation, review and feedback, and onboarding/offboarding.
- Regulatory convergence (EU AI Act, Singapore's IMDA, Australia's mandatory AI standards) makes documented role definition, decision rights, and oversight both a strategic and a compliance imperative.

---

## Summary
The article argues that agentic AI is no longer a future concern: agents are already making decisions inside organisations, frequently embedded within vendor SaaS products rather than deliberately deployed, and they are spreading faster than governance can keep pace. It cites McKinsey (62% of organisations had experimented with agentic AI by end-2025, 23% scaling in business functions) and IBM (99% of enterprise developers exploring or building agents) to establish that the exposure is current and widespread. Boards, the author contends, must stop treating this as a technology roadmap item and instead assess their existing exposure and accountability structures now.

The central reframing is a move from governing AI as a *tool* to governing it as a *partner*. Tool governance focuses on accuracy and is controlled through prompts; partner governance evaluates judgment and is exercised through grants of authority and explicit constraints — the appropriate posture once an agent can perceive, decide, and act within a defined role. The author grounds this in the concept of *functional equality* (agents fulfilling a defined role to the required standard alongside humans, not cognitive equivalence) and references the 2005 TeamMATE research on artificial agents as genuine collaborators in shared decision-making. The practical test of whether real governance exists is the Delegation Test: can the organisation articulate the same role, authority, oversight, and accountability for an agent that it would for a human hire?

The recommendations are concrete and time-boxed. A 90-day action plan calls for discovering the agents already operating (including those embedded in vendor systems), assigning individual human accountability for each agent's decisions, testing high-risk agents against the Delegation Test, and establishing a defensible baseline of governance. Each agent should be evaluated against eight management questions spanning its role and instructions, the policies and procedures it must follow, the decisions and authority boundaries it is granted, its escalation behaviour when out of mandate, its management and review mechanisms, and the conditions for onboarding and offboarding. With global regulation converging on documented decision rights and oversight, the author concludes that competitive and compliance advantage will belong to organisations that establish governance ahead of deployment rather than defending, after the fact, decisions already made by unmonitored systems.
