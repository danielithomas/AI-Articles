# Meeting the Challenge of Disruptive Change

**Authors:** Clayton M. Christensen and Michael Overdorf

**Category:** Strategy & Governance

**Published:** March–April 2000

---

## Key Points
- Established firms fail at disruptive change not because they lack capable people, but because their processes and values are tuned to the existing business and mismatched to the new task.
- The Resources, Processes, and Values (RPV) framework explains what an organisation can and cannot do: resources are flexible, but processes and values are far harder to change.
- Capabilities migrate over time from resources (people, who are adaptable) into processes and values (which become rigid and define the organisation's culture), so the very maturity that makes a firm good at its core business disables it elsewhere.
- The same processes and values that drive success at sustaining innovation actively disincline a firm from pursuing disruptive innovation, because new markets look too small, too uncertain, and too low-margin to merit resource allocation.
- Managers should match the organisational vehicle to the task — acquire a firm whose processes and values already fit, create a heavyweight team or separate unit inside the company, or spin out an independent organisation — depending on how well the challenge fits existing processes and values.

---

## Summary
Christensen and Overdorf address why successful, well-managed companies so often fail when confronted with disruptive change. Their answer is that managers habitually assess whether they have the right *people* for a job but rarely ask whether the *organisation* is capable of it. To make that assessable, they introduce the Resources, Processes, and Values (RPV) framework. Resources — people, capital, technology, brands, relationships — are the most visible and the most transferable. Processes are the established patterns of interaction, coordination, and decision-making by which resources are turned into products and services; they are deliberately designed not to change, which makes them efficient at their intended task but inflexible elsewhere. Values are the criteria by which employees prioritise decisions — which customers matter, which margins are acceptable, which opportunities are big enough to pursue. Together, processes and values define what an organisation cannot do as much as what it can.

A central insight is that capabilities migrate. In a young company, what gets done depends heavily on its resources (its people), so the firm can flex to many tasks. As it matures, capabilities shift into processes and values, which become embedded and self-reinforcing — and far less adaptable. This explains the paradox of disruptive innovation: the processes and values that make a leading firm excellent at sustaining innovations (serving its best customers with better products at good margins) are precisely what make it incapable of pursuing disruptive ones, which initially serve small, unproven markets at lower margins. The organisation's values cause it to starve disruptive opportunities of resources, and its processes have no routine for the new task. The failure is structural and predictable, not a matter of poor management or weak talent.

The authors' prescription is to deliberately create new capabilities by choosing the right organisational structure for the disruption at hand: acquire an organisation whose existing processes and values match the new task; develop new processes inside the company through a heavyweight, dedicated team that works across functional boundaries; or spin out an independent organisation with values appropriate to the disruptive market when the mainstream business would otherwise smother it. Although written in 2000 with no reference to AI, the argument is strikingly applicable to enterprise AI adoption: it explains why incumbents with abundant resources (talent, capital, data) still struggle to absorb a disruptive technology, and offers a concrete framework for diagnosing organisational fit and structuring AI initiatives so that mismatched processes and values do not quietly kill them.
