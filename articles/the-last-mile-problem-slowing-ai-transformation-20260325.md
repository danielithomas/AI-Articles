# The "Last Mile" Problem Slowing AI Transformation
**Authors:** Karim R. Lakhani, Jared Spataro, Jen Stave

**Category:** Strategy & Governance

**Published:** 9 March 2026

---

## Key Points
- Most organisations are "pilot-rich but transformation-poor" — hundreds of successful AI pilots fail to scale into enterprise-wide operating models due to the absence of repeatable pathways from proof-of-concept to deployment.
- Individual productivity gains from AI tools remain trapped inside individual workflows and do not translate to organisational results without intentional role redesign and budget reallocation.
- Legacy "process debt" — brittle, exception-ridden workflows accumulated over decades — is exposed faster by AI than it can be resolved, requiring fundamental process re-architecture rather than AI overlays.
- Long-tenured employees resist externalising undocumented tribal knowledge into AI systems because that expertise is tied to their professional identity, creating an identity-driven adoption barrier.
- The authors propose an "AI-native firm" blueprint centred on clean-sheet process redesign, strategic knowledge capture, centralised agentic governance via control planes, and new human roles focused on design, orchestration, and interpretation.

---

## Summary
Karim Lakhani (Harvard Business School), Jared Spataro (Microsoft), and Jen Stave (Harvard Digital Data Design Institute) argue that despite massive AI investment and hundreds of successful pilots, the primary obstacle to enterprise AI transformation is not technological but organisational. Drawing on cases from investment banking, food and beverage, healthcare insurance, payments, and apparel industries, they identify seven structural frictions that stall the "last mile" between pilot success and scaled transformation. These include a proliferation of disconnected pilots, a productivity gap where individual gains fail to lift organisational performance, and process debt where AI exposes legacy workflow fragility faster than it can resolve it.

Three of the seven frictions are distinctly human and governance-related. First, an identity problem: long-tenured employees whose professional standing rests on undocumented tribal knowledge resist encoding that judgment into systems, viewing it as a threat to their value. Second, an agentic governance gap: traditional software governance structures are inadequate for multi-agent architectures that require treating agents as a managed workforce with accountability, permissions, and performance monitoring. Third, an efficiency trap: framing AI narrowly as cost reduction triggers defensive behaviour and risks eliminating the judgment and storytelling capabilities that differentiate high-value work.

The authors prescribe an "AI-native firm" blueprint comprising four pillars. Clean-sheet process redesign asks "if we were building this process today with modern AI agents, how would we do it?" rather than layering AI onto legacy systems. Strategic knowledge capture frames the externalisation of expert judgment as "legacy building" rather than reskilling, securing buy-in from knowledge holders. Centralised agentic control planes monitor agent performance, permissions, and accuracy analogous to HR performance management. Finally, deliberate role redesign shifts humans toward design, orchestration, and interpretation, creating new positions such as AI process architects and knowledge stewards to prevent adoption from feeling like career erosion.
