# Maximising AI Investments: Beyond Individual Productivity

**Authors:** Daniel Thomas (PhD) and Duncan Unwin

**Category:** Strategy & Governance

**Published:** 19 August 2025

---

## Key Points
- Organisations typically pursue AI for individual productivity gains, but these deliver limited and unreliable returns at the firm level.
- Gartner's research indicates individual productivity enhancements do not reliably translate into significant firm-level gains without strategic alignment.
- The productivity paradox (Brynjolfsson and McAfee) shows heavy technology investment often yields inconsistent performance improvements unless paired with complementary innovation and organisational alignment.
- The authors propose a three-horizon portfolio: short-term system-level automation and forecasting, mid-term KPI transformation and scenario planning, and long-term AI-enabled innovation creating competitive advantage and new revenue.
- Leaders should balance AI initiatives as a portfolio across horizons rather than choosing between cost reduction and visionary goals, with CIOs auditing initiatives and aligning them to long-term business objectives.

---

## Summary
The article argues that most organisations frame their AI investments too narrowly — around individual productivity gains from consumer tools such as ChatGPT — and that this focus systematically under-delivers at the level that matters: the firm. Drawing on Gartner's finding that individual productivity enhancements do not reliably translate into significant firm-level gains, the authors contend that executives who stop at personal efficiency miss the larger pool of value that AI can unlock when it is connected to strategy.

To explain why technology spend so often disappoints, the authors invoke the productivity paradox documented by Brynjolfsson and McAfee: heavy investment in technology frequently produces inconsistent performance improvements because the technology alone is not enough. Realising returns requires complementary innovation — changes to processes, capabilities, and organisational design — and genuine strategic alignment. The central concepts the piece foregrounds are complementary innovation, system-level efficiency, and strategic alignment as the bridge between raw technological capability and organisational value.

The recommended response is a diversified, portfolio-based approach structured across three time horizons. In the short term, system-level automation and forecasting deliver immediate operational savings; in the mid term, KPI transformation and scenario planning enable smarter capital allocation and decision-making; and in the long term, AI-enabled innovation fosters durable competitive advantage and new revenue streams. Rather than treating cost reduction and visionary transformation as a binary choice, leaders should hold a balanced portfolio that spans all three. The authors call on CIOs to evaluate their current initiatives, identify where greater strategic clarity is possible, and work with executive teams to align AI investment with long-term business objectives.
